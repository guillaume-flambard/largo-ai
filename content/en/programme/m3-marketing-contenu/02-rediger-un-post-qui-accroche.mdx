You have your angles (lesson 1). Now to turn them into posts that don't go
unnoticed. An effective post comes down to little: a good **hook**, a clear
**body**, a simple **call to action**. AI can produce all three — your job is to
steer it and keep your voice.

## The key idea

<Idee>
A post is won in the **first two lines.** If the hook doesn't draw people in, the
rest is never read. So ask the AI to **polish the hook first** — and even to
offer you several, because that's where attention is won or lost.
</Idee>

## The three beats of a post

- **The hook** — a sentence that sparks curiosity, poses a problem or promises an
  answer. *"Most flowerbeds that die in summer aren't short of water."*
- **The body** — the answer, short, concrete, airy. One idea per paragraph.
- **The call to action** — what the reader does next: a question, an invitation
  to comment, a contact.

<Exemple>
*"From this angle [paste the angle from lesson 1], write a LinkedIn post: an
attention-grabbing hook, 3 concrete points, a closing question to start the
discussion. Professional but human tone, no jargon, short paragraphs."*
</Exemple>

## The right tone for the right place

<Attention>
The same content isn't said the same way everywhere: LinkedIn calls for a calm,
professional tone, Instagram something more direct and visual, a newsletter a
more personal tone. Always specify the **network**: otherwise the AI produces an
"average" tone that fits nowhere. And beware the AI tics that are very noticeable
on social media (emojis on every line, "Here's why 👇", grandiose phrasing).
</Attention>

## Spin to choose

<Idee>
With a post, the most useful thing isn't to get ONE version, but **three
different hooks** for the same angle. You compare, you sense which one rings
true, and you keep the best — even mixing them. Choosing between options is
easier, and safer, than judging a version in isolation.
</Idee>

## Keep your voice (again)

As with email (module M2), the risk is uniformity. Give the AI one or two of your
previous posts that sound like you: *"Write in this style."* And reread aloud — if
you'd never say it that way, tweak it.

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Take an angle from your stock (lesson 1). Ask the AI, for a specific network:
**3 different hooks** + a body in 3 points + a closing question.

Choose the hook that grabs **you**, assemble the post, then reread it aloud. Tweak
anything that doesn't sound like you. In ten minutes, you have a post ready — and
the habit for the next ones.
</Exercice>

## In short

- A post is won on the **hook** (the first two lines); have **several** produced.
- Simple structure: **hook → clear body → call to action.**
- Specify the **network** and the **tone**; avoid recognisable AI tics.
- **Keep your voice**: an example of your posts as a model, and a read-aloud.

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min on the weight of the hook · 4 min on the three
beats with a post generated live · 3 min on tone by network · 2 min on spinning
versions · 4 min exercise.

**Hook workshop.** The most instructive moment: take an angle and generate 3
hooks on screen, then have the group vote. You see concretely why one hook
"works" and another falls flat — more telling than any theory.

**Frequent questions**
- *"How many posts a week?"* → regularity beats frequency. Better one good post a
  week, sustained, than five over ten days then nothing. We discuss rhythm in
  lesson 4.
- *"Should I use emojis / hashtags?"* → depending on the network and your style;
  in moderation. The trap is the "over-optimised" post that reeks of AI.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't praise clickbait hooks ("You won't believe…"). They
work once then wear out trust. An honest, concrete hook lasts — consistent with
the M1 mindset.
</Formateur>

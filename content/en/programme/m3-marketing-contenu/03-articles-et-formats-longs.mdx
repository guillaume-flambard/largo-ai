A post is read in ten seconds; an article, a website page or a tip sheet takes
several minutes to read. Long-form needs a different method: you don't ask for it
"all at once", you **build** it. And this is where vigilance about facts becomes
central again.

## The key idea

<Idee>
For long-form, never ask for the whole article in one go: you'd get a padded,
smooth text that's hard to fix. Work in two stages: **the outline first** (which
you validate and adjust), **then the writing section by section.** You keep
control of the structure and the accuracy.
</Idee>

## The two-stage method

<Exemple>
**Stage 1 — the outline:** *"I want an article for my private clients: 'How to
prepare your garden for winter'. Suggest an outline in 5 sections, from urgent to
optional, geared toward practical advice."* → you read, reorganise, cut whatever
doesn't sound like you.

**Stage 2 — the writing:** *"Write section 2 'Protecting fragile plants',
concrete and accessible tone, with an example."* → section by section, you stay
in charge of the substance.
</Exemple>

## The long-form trap: padding

<Attention>
Over length, the AI has two flaws: it **pads** (many sentences for few ideas) and
it **buries invented facts** in the mass — a statistic, a date, an unfounded
"wellness" claim, all the harder to spot because there's text around them. Two
habits: ask for **shorter and denser** ("cut the filler, keep the concrete"), and
**check every factual claim** as in module M1.
</Attention>

## Getting found: the basics, no jargon

An article only matters if it's found. No need to become an SEO expert; two
simple principles are enough at this stage:

<Idee>
Write the **title and subheadings with the words your client would type** into a
search engine. Someone searching doesn't type "an autumn ode to the garden" but
"prepare your garden for winter". The AI can help: *"Suggest 5 titles for this
article, with the words private individuals would actually search for."* The rest
— clearly answering a real question — is already your best asset.
</Idee>

## Keep your added value

Long-form is where your expertise shows. The AI structures and writes, but it's
**you** who bring the trade details, the lived examples, the warnings no one else
gives. An article that contains only "general AI knowledge" is recognisable and
brings nothing. Slip in what only you know.

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Choose a question your clients often ask that deserves more than a post. **Stage
1:** have an outline of 4-5 sections generated, and **rework it** (order,
additions, cuts). **Stage 2:** have **one section** written, then scrutinise it:
too padded? a fact to check? a trade detail to add?

You've got the method; the other sections will follow the same path.
</Exercice>

## In short

- Long-form = **outline first** (validated), **writing section by section** next.
- Watch for **padding** and **invented facts** buried in the length.
- To get found: **title and subheadings with the client's words.**
- Your **real expertise** is what sets the article apart from generic content.

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min on "why not all at once" · 5 min on the
two-stage method, outline generated and reworked live · 3 min on padding +
checking · 3 min on the "findable" title · 2 min launching the exercise.

**Show the contrast.** Generate the article "all at once" first, then the "outline
+ section" version. The group sees the difference in quality and control — the
argument needs no speech.

**Frequent questions**
- *"SEO is more complicated than that, isn't it?"* → yes, it's a profession; but
  at this level, truly answering a real question with the right words in the
  title is already the essential. Don't overload the lesson.
- *"How many words for a good article?"* → enough to answer, no more. Length
  isn't a goal; usefulness is.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't let people think you "publish what the AI puts out."
Long-form is precisely where rereading and personal input are indispensable. The
longer it is, the more you check.
</Formateur>

The hardest part of content isn't writing: it's **knowing what to say**,
regularly, without repeating yourself or going hollow. This is exactly where AI
is a valuable partner — provided you feed it your trade, rather than letting it
churn out the generic.

## The key idea

<Idee>
The AI doesn't know your trade, your clients, your concrete cases — you bring
those. Its role isn't to invent your ideas for you, but to **multiply and sort**
the material you give it: spin one topic into ten angles, turn a client question
into a post theme. You provide the real, it provides the volume.
</Idee>

## Start from your material, not the void

A "give me some post ideas" request yields platitudes. A well-fed request yields
gold:

<Exemple>
*"I'm a landscape gardener, working mostly for private clients. My clients often
ask me the same questions before a project (budget, upkeep, seasons). Suggest 10
post ideas that answer these questions, each with a concrete angle and an example
from a real job."*

The best content ideas are almost always **the questions your clients already
ask you.**
</Exemple>

## From topic to angle

A topic ("garden upkeep") isn't content. An **angle** is: a precise question, a
viewpoint, a case. The angle is what makes someone stop scrolling.

<Exemple>
- Soft topic: *"The importance of watering."*
- Sharp angles: *"The 3 watering mistakes that kill a flowerbed in summer"* ·
  *"Why your lawn yellows even when you water a lot"* · *"How long to water,
  really, depending on your soil."*

Ask the AI to turn each topic into **three concrete angles**, and choose.
</Exemple>

## Building a stock

<Idee>
Producing regularly becomes easy when you never start from zero. Once a month,
have 15-20 angles generated from your client questions and recent jobs, and
**keep the list.** When it's time to publish, you pick from it: the blank-page
dread disappears, because the "what to say" decision is already made.
</Idee>

## Keep the reality filter

<Attention>
The AI may suggest appealing angles that are **wrong for you**: an invented
statistic, a "trend" that doesn't match your clientele, a tone that isn't yours.
Stay in control: only keep the angles you can **own and illustrate with a real
example.** Good content rests on what you actually know, not on what sounds good.
</Attention>

## Your turn

<Exercice>
In one request, give the AI: **your trade + your clientele + 3 questions your
clients often ask.** Ask for **10 content ideas**, each narrowed to a **precise
angle** illustrable with a real case.

In the list, circle the **three** you could write tomorrow with a concrete
example. You're holding the start of your stock.
</Exercice>

## In short

- The AI **multiplies and sorts** your ideas; the material (trade, clients,
  cases) comes from **you.**
- The best ideas = **the questions your clients already ask.**
- Move from the **topic** (vague) to the **angle** (precise, illustrable).
- Build a **stock of angles** so you never start from zero — keeping only what
  you can **own.**

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min framing (the real problem = what to say) ·
4 min starting from your material, with a well-fed request live · 4 min topic →
angle (the 3-angles exercise) · 5 min generating angles on a participant's trade.

**An effective demo.** Take a participant's trade, ask for their 3 recurring
client questions, and generate the angles on screen. The group sees their own
activity become a content mine — far more motivating than an abstract example.

**Frequent questions**
- *"What if I have no idea about client questions?"* → go back to the last emails
  or calls received: the real questions are already there. It's the best source.
- *"Won't the AI make me publish like everyone else?"* → only if you keep the
  generic. The angle drawn from YOUR cases is what sets you apart; we work on
  brand voice in lesson 4.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't turn the session into a volume race ("50 post
ideas!"). Three solid angles, rooted in the real, beat fifty hollow ones. The
quality of the angle matters more than quantity.
</Formateur>

<Attention>
This module is **awareness-raising**, not legal advice. It gives you the right
reflexes and vocabulary to talk to a professional. For your precise situation —
and because the rules evolve — rely on an official source (in France, the CNIL) or
specialist advice.
</Attention>

The first building block of compliance isn't a law: it's a habit. Before talking
about the GDPR or the AI Act, let's set the simplest and most protective move:
**paying attention to what you give the AI.**

## The key idea

<Idee>
What you enter into a consumer AI **may, depending on the tool and its settings,
be stored or used** to improve the service. So start from a cautious principle:
**don't entrust an AI with information you wouldn't say in public** — clients'
personal data, financial details, trade secrets, passwords or credentials.
</Idee>

## The dividing line

- **You can go ahead freely** — already-public texts, generic content, method
  questions, drafts with no real data.
- **You refrain or anonymise** — clients' names and contact details, medical or
  social records, confidential company figures, contracts, login credentials.

<Exemple>
**To avoid:** *"Here's my client list with their addresses and revenue, give me an
analysis."*

**Better:** replace the real names with "Client A, Client B", round off or
replace the amounts, and ask for the same analysis on this anonymised data. You
keep the AI's benefit without exposing anyone.
</Exemple>

## Anonymising: the reflex that unlocks everything

<Idee>
Anonymisation is your best ally: it lets you use AI on real cases **without
passing on identifying information.** Replace names with labels (Client A,
Supplier X), neutralise sensitive figures, remove anything that identifies a
person. Often, the AI doesn't need the real data to help you with the **form** or
the **method.**
</Idee>

## Know your tools' settings

<Attention>
AI tools often offer **privacy settings**: turning off the use of your exchanges
for training, deleting history, or switching to a professional plan with stronger
guarantees. Take five minutes to find them in the tools you use. Never assume a
tool is confidential "by default": check. And beware of unknown free tools, where
you don't know what they do with your data.
</Attention>

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Take a real task where you'd hesitate to use AI because it involves sensitive data
(a client file, an internal document).

1. **Anonymise it**: list what you'd replace (names, figures, contact details) to
   be able to entrust it to AI safely.
2. **Open the privacy settings** of your main AI tool and find the data-use
   option. Decide your setting.

In ten minutes, you turn a zone of worry into controlled use.
</Exercice>

## In short

- What you enter **may be stored/reused**: entrust nothing you wouldn't say in
  public.
- Dividing line: public/generic = OK; **personal, confidential data, credentials
  = avoid or anonymise.**
- **Anonymisation** lets you use AI on real cases without exposing anyone.
- **Set the privacy** of your tools; never assume "confidential by default."

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 1 min framing (awareness, not legal) · 4 min on the
dividing line with examples · 4 min on anonymisation live · 4 min on tool settings
· 2 min launching the exercise.

**Set the tone without scaring.** The risk of this module is to frighten people
into not using AI at all. Hold the M1 balance: we use AI **a lot**, keeping our
eyes open. Anonymisation is good news ("you can go ahead"), not a constraint.

**Useful demonstration.** Anonymise a real case on screen (a client email) and
show that the AI helps just as well on the anonymised version. The group sees it
almost never needs to expose the real data.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Are professional plans safer?"* → they often offer stronger guarantees (data
  not reused, hosting); to check case by case in the tool's terms. Don't promise,
  point.
- *"What if I've already pasted sensitive data?"* → a chance to look at the
  history and deletion options; and to adjust your settings going forward. Defuse,
  fix.

**Pitfall to avoid (facilitator):** don't give clear-cut legal advice, even when
asked. Point to official sources and the following lessons. Your role is
awareness, not consultancy.
</Formateur>

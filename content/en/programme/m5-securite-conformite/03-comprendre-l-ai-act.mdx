<Attention>
As in the previous lessons: **awareness-raising, not legal advice.** The AI Act is
a recent and evolving framework; here we give its spirit and reflexes, not a
detailed reading. For your exact obligations, refer to official sources and, if
needed, a specialist.
</Attention>

The AI Act is the European regulation on artificial intelligence. No need to know
it by heart: what matters for you is to grasp its **spirit** and to **locate your
use** in the landscape. That's largely enough to work with peace of mind.

## The key idea

<Idee>
The AI Act rests on a simple idea: **regulate AI in proportion to its risk to
people.** The more a use can affect people's lives (their rights, their safety,
their opportunities), the stronger the requirements. Common, low-risk uses, on the
other hand, are very lightly constrained.
</Idee>

## A logic by risk levels

Without going into detail, the spirit of the text roughly distinguishes:

- **Unacceptable** uses, banned as contrary to fundamental rights.
- **High-risk** uses (e.g. those that weigh heavily on people's lives), subject to
  strict obligations.
- **Limited-risk** uses, where **transparency** is mainly expected.
- **Minimal-risk** uses — the vast majority of daily work — very lightly
  regulated.

<Exemple>
Using AI to draft an email, summarise a note or create a visual is a
**minimal-risk** use. By contrast, relying on AI to **screen job applications** or
evaluate people touches on strong human stakes: that's where caution and,
potentially, obligations come into play.
</Exemple>

## Where do you usually stand?

<Idee>
As a small business, you're almost always a **user** of existing AI tools, not a
**maker** of an AI system. Your common uses (writing, summarising, creating
content) are low-risk. The framework becomes a serious matter mainly if you use AI
to **decide people's fate** (employment, access to a service, evaluation). Knowing
how to recognise these situations is the key point.
</Idee>

## The reflex that counts: transparency

<Attention>
Beyond formal obligations, keep one simple and always-healthy principle:
**transparency.** When content or an exchange is produced or heavily assisted by AI
and it may matter to the person you're dealing with, say so. Passing off an AI text
as purely human work, or a chatbot as a person, erodes trust — and goes against the
spirit of the text. Being honest about AI use is both compliant and good for your
reputation.
</Attention>

## Your turn

<Exercice>
List **three uses** of AI in your work (real or planned). For each, ask yourself:
*does this directly affect people's lives or rights?*

- If **no** (writing, content, organisation): low-risk use, carry on with peace of
  mind.
- If **yes** (selection, evaluation, decisions about people): mark it "to dig
  into" — that's where specialist advice is warranted.

For content meant for others, add a note: *should the use of AI be disclosed?*
</Exercice>

## In short

- The AI Act regulates AI **by risk** to people; common daily work is **lightly
  concerned.**
- You're mostly a **user** of existing tools: vigilance rises when AI **decides
  people's fate.**
- The universal reflex: **transparency** about AI use when it matters.
- For high human-stakes uses: **do your homework, seek advice.**

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 1 min framing (spirit, not detail) · 4 min on the
risk-based logic with examples · 4 min on "user vs maker" and where you stand ·
4 min on transparency · 2 min exercise.

**Reassure, then target.** The two-part message: "for your common uses, breathe,
it's lightly constrained"; "but know how to recognise high human-stakes uses."
This distinction avoids both panic and carelessness.

**Avoid the expertise trap.** The AI Act is technical and shifting. Don't claim to
master it; convey the spirit and the caution reflex. Prepare an up-to-date official
source to recommend.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Does it apply already?"* → the framework is rolling out gradually; the spirit,
  though, is already a good guide. For precise deadlines, point to official sources
  (they move).
- *"Do I have to disclose AI on all my content?"* → not mechanically; mainly when
  the absence of disclosure would mislead the other person. Common sense and
  honesty come first.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't turn the lesson into a European law course. Three
reflexes (proportionality to risk, user vs maker, transparency) are amply enough at
this level.
</Formateur>

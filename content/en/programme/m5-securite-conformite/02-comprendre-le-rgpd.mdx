<Attention>
Reminder: this is **awareness-raising**, not legal advice. The principles below
are deliberately general. For your precise case, rely on an official source (in
France, the CNIL) or a professional.
</Attention>

The GDPR often scares people by its acronym. Yet its spirit comes down to a
common-sense idea: **information about people deserves respect and caution.** When
you use AI, a few simple principles are enough to stay on the right side — or to
know when to ask for help.

## The key idea

<Idee>
The GDPR governs the processing of **personal data** — any information relating to
an identifiable person (a name, an email, a phone number, but also a photo, an
identifier, sometimes a simple combination of details). As soon as you handle this
kind of data with AI, these principles apply, whatever the size of your outfit.
</Idee>

## A few simple principles to keep in mind

Without going into the legal detail, here's the spirit of the rules, useful day to
day:

- **Minimisation** — pass on only the data **necessary** for the task. That's
  exactly the anonymisation from lesson 1.
- **Purpose** — use data for a **precise, stated purpose**, not "because we have
  it."
- **Transparency** — the people concerned should be able to know what you do with
  their data.
- **Retention** — don't keep indefinitely what you no longer need.
- **Processors** — an AI tool that processes data for you becomes a kind of
  provider: you must make sure of its guarantees.

<Exemple>
You want to analyse client reviews with AI. A GDPR-respectful approach: remove the
names (minimisation), use these reviews only to improve the service (purpose), via
a tool you've checked doesn't reuse the data (processor). The same work, done
cleanly.
</Exemple>

## The sensitive point: where does the data go?

<Attention>
With AI, one question always comes back: **where is the data processed, and by
whom?** Many tools process information on servers outside your country, sometimes
outside the European Union, which adds requirements. You don't have to master
everything, but to **be aware** of the question — and to ask it before entrusting
personal data to a tool. In case of serious doubt, that's precisely the moment to
seek advice.
</Attention>

## Knowing when to ask for help

<Idee>
Awareness has a healthy limit. If your AI use involves **a lot** of personal data,
**sensitive** data (health, social situation, opinions), or **systematic**
processing, don't stay alone: read up via official sources and, if needed, get
support. Recognising that you need a specialist is a skill, not an admission of
weakness.
</Idee>

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Think of **one AI use** you have (or are considering) involving people's data. Run
it through the principles:

1. **Minimisation** — what data could you remove without losing the point?
2. **Purpose** — is the goal clear and legitimate?
3. **Where does the data go** — do you know which tool processes it, and where?

If an answer makes you uneasy, note it: that's a point to clarify (by you, or with
an adviser).
</Exercice>

## In short

- **Personal data** = any info about an identifiable person; the GDPR then
  applies, **whatever your size.**
- Spirit of the rules: **minimisation, purpose, transparency, limited retention,
  processor guarantees.**
- Always ask **where the data goes.**
- For **heavy or sensitive** uses, do your homework and **seek specialist
  advice.**

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 1 min framing (awareness) · 3 min on "personal data" ·
5 min on the principles with the client-reviews example · 3 min on "where does the
data go" · 3 min filtering exercise.

**Stay concrete.** The trap is reciting the GDPR like a law course. Instead: bring
each principle back to an AI move already seen (minimisation = anonymisation from
M5L1). The group should leave with reflexes, not articles.

**Know your limits as facilitator.** You're not a lawyer (unless you are). Own it:
the value is making the topic approachable and knowing how to point. Prepare the
contact details of official sources to give.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Is a micro-business really concerned?"* → yes, the GDPR doesn't depend on
  size. But the expected effort is proportionate to the use: a baker managing a
  client file doesn't have a platform's obligations.
- *"Can AI help me be compliant?"* → it can explain principles, help draft an
  information notice… but it isn't authoritative on the law (M1 reflex: check). It
  informs, it doesn't decide.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't give definitive legal answers. On a sharp question,
the good facilitator answer is often: "good question, here's where to find the
reliable answer."
</Formateur>

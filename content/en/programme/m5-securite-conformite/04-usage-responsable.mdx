<Attention>
A final reminder: this module is **awareness-raising**, not legal advice. For your
precise situation — and because the rules evolve — rely on an official source (in
France, the CNIL) or specialist advice.
</Attention>

This module ends where it began: with **reflexes**, not articles of law. Security,
GDPR, AI Act come down, day to day, to a responsible posture you can hold without
being a lawyer. This final lesson turns it into concrete habits — and into an
asset.

## The key idea

<Idee>
Responsible AI use doesn't require a legal department: it requires **a few simple
rules, shared and upheld.** Protect data, check what matters, stay transparent,
keep the human on decisions: these reflexes, written down and applied, are enough
to cover the bulk of the risk for a small outfit.
</Idee>

## Setting yourself a mini AI charter

No need for a complex document. One page shared with your team (or for yourself) is
enough. A few useful rules, to adapt:

<Exemple>
- We **don't enter** identifying client data or confidential information into a
  consumer AI; we **anonymise** if needed.
- We **check** facts (figures, dates, references, law) before any external use.
- We **keep** the human decision on anything that affects people.
- We **disclose** AI use when honesty calls for it.
- In case of serious doubt, we **seek advice** rather than improvise.
</Exemple>

This charter isn't a straitjacket: it's a shared reference point that prevents
mistakes and reassures everyone.

## The often-forgotten point: bias

<Attention>
AI isn't neutral. It learned on huge amounts of human text — and can **reproduce
stereotypes** (of gender, origin, age…) or questionable angles present in its
data. On sensitive topics — a text about people, a selection, a delicate
communication — keep a **critical human eye.** Ask yourself: *does this result
treat everyone fairly?* Vigilance about bias is part of responsible use.
</Attention>

## Responsibility as an asset

<Idee>
Done well, compliance isn't just a protection: it's an **argument for trust.**
Telling your clients "here's how we use AI, and how we protect your data" sets you
apart, especially with a wary audience. Transparency and seriousness become a
commercial advantage, not a cost.
</Idee>

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Write your **mini AI charter in 5 points maximum**, from the module's reflexes:
data, checking, human decision, transparency, recourse to advice. Phrase it
simply, like rules you could post on a wall.

Then test the bias reflex: ask the AI for an example on a human topic (e.g.
describe a "good candidate" for a role) and **reread it critically** — are there
implicit assumptions to correct? You've got your framework for responsible use.
</Exercice>

## In short

- Responsible use = **a few simple rules, shared and upheld**, not a legal
  department.
- A **mini charter** (data, checking, human decision, transparency, recourse)
  covers the essential.
- AI can reproduce **bias**: keep a **critical human eye** on sensitive topics.
- Well explained, responsibility is an **asset of trust**, not a constraint.

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min module synthesis (data → GDPR → AI Act →
responsibility) · 4 min on the mini charter, written live · 4 min on bias with a
revealing example · 2 min on responsibility-as-asset · 3 min exercise.

**Landing the module and the programme.** This is the last lesson of the common
track: everyone should leave with **their mini charter** drafted. It's M5's
concrete commitment, as each module has its own. Have it written in session.

**Bias demonstration.** Generate live a description of a "good candidate" or a
visual of a "manager" and observe the implicit assumptions together. It's often a
strong moment: the group sees concretely that AI isn't neutral, with no
campaigning tone.

**Closing the programme's posture.** The whole common track (M1→M5) rests on one
posture: using AI with benefit **and** clear sight. Naming it one last time helps
the group leave with a coherent vision, not a list of tools.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Isn't a charter too much for a small team?"* → five posted lines are enough;
  the point is sharing, not formalism. Better one upheld page than an ignored
  binder.
- *"How do I stay up to date?"* → follow an official source, and remember that the
  reflexes (data, checking, transparency) hold even as the texts evolve.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't end the programme on an anxious note. The last message
should be empowering: you have the reflexes to use AI responsibly — and that makes
you more trustworthy, not more timid.
</Formateur>

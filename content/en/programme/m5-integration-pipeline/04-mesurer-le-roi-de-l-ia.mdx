# Measuring AI ROI

"AI saves us time" is an intuition. "Spec-to-merge cycle time dropped from 3.2 days to 1.8 days over the last quarter" is a measurement. The difference between the two is what enables arbitration: where to keep going, where to reduce usage, where AI creates more problems than it solves.

## What to measure and what not to measure

Metrics that measure the wrong thing:

- **Lines of code generated**: correlated with zero actual quality or velocity
- **Number of prompts sent**: an activity indicator, not an outcome indicator
- **Subjective sentiment** ("I feel more productive"): biased by the novelty effect

Metrics that reveal something useful:

- **Spec → merge cycle time**: from when a task is defined to when it passes CI
- **Re-open rate**: bugs raised after merge on AI-first features vs. classic development
- **Review cost**: human review time per PR (if AI generates larger, less readable PRs, ROI is negative)
- **Token cost** vs. value delivered: for automated workflows (n8n + LLM), how much are you spending per month to automate what?

<Idee>
Measure over a minimum of one sprint, excluding the first two weeks of adoption (ramp-up). A developer who has mastered the AI-first workflow does not have the same velocity as one who is learning it — comparing the two periods gives a biased signal.
</Idee>

## A measurement protocol for a team

**Baseline (before)**: extract from your management tool (Linear, Jira, GitHub) the average cycle time for features over the last 3 sprints. Also note the post-merge bug rate and the number of review comments per PR.

**After adoption**: measure the same metrics over 2 full sprints. Compare feature by feature where possible — some types of tasks benefit more than others (simple CRUD forms: large gain; complex algorithms: moderate gain; architectural refactors: sometimes negative).

**What deserves a table:**

| Task | Before (h) | After (h) | Delta | ROI |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Simple CRUD endpoint | 4h | 1.5h | −63% | ✓ strong |
| Feature with complex business logic | 8h | 6h | −25% | ✓ moderate |
| Cross-layer refactor | 12h | 14h | +17% | ✗ negative |
| DB schema migration | 6h | 2h | −67% | ✓ strong |

<Exemple>
**Typical monthly report — team of 4 devs, 2 months after adoption:**

- Spec→merge cycle time: 3.1d → 1.9d (−39%)
- Re-open rate: 12% → 9% (−3 pts — slight improvement)
- Review comments per PR: 8.2 → 11.4 (+39% — PRs are denser, review is heavier)
- Anthropic token cost: ~€180/month for the team

**Decisions made**: keep the workflow for CRUD + tests; add a systematic PR splitting step for AI-first PRs (they are too large); do not use the agent on architectural refactors.
</Exemple>

## The areas where AI degrades

Blind adoption produces documented negative effects. The most common:

**Oversized PRs**: the agent generates in one session what a developer would have naturally split up. Result: 800-line diffs impossible to review seriously.

**False confidence on test coverage**: high coverage generated automatically, but implementation tests with no real value (see M4).

**Context debt**: `CLAUDE.md` files and specs become unmaintained artefacts. After 3 months, the agent is working from stale context and produces inconsistent results.

**Token overhead on simple tasks**: automating with an LLM what could be solved in 5 lines of code or a trivial business rule.

<Attention>
AI ROI is not uniform over time. The typical curve: strong gains at adoption (simple, well-scoped tasks), plateau after 6 weeks, then risk of degradation if context files are not maintained. Schedule a review of `CLAUDE.md` files and agent conventions every 2 sprints.
</Attention>

<Exercice>
Pick three recent features developed partially or fully AI-first. For each: estimate the real development time (commits, PRs, reviews). Compare to your estimate for an equivalent classic feature. Calculate the delta. Is there a pattern in the types of tasks where the gain is real vs. absent? Share the results with the team — collective arbitration is more reliable than individual measurement.
</Exercice>

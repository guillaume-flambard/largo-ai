You know how to spot, build an assistant, connect your tools. What remains is the
one question that really matters: **is it actually saving you time?** An
automation only has value if the gain is real, net of the checking it requires.
This closing lesson teaches you to measure it and keep control.

## The key idea

<Idee>
The gain from an automation is the time of the task **minus** the time to set it
up (spread over its uses) **minus** the time to check the results. An automation
that forces you to recheck everything gains nothing: it just shifts the work. The
goal isn't to automate for the sake of it, but to **free up net time.**
</Idee>

## Measure for real

As in module M1, you don't rely on impressions. A simple measure is enough:

<Exemple>
For a task you've just automated, compare over a few cases:

- **Before**: how long did it take, done by hand?
- **After**: how long does it take now, **checking included**?

If you used to spend 10 minutes and now spend 2 (including 1 of rereading), the
gain is net and the automation is justified. If you spend 8 minutes fixing what it
produced, it's useless.
</Exemple>

## A proportionate safeguard

<Attention>
Every automation needs a **human checkpoint** — but calibrated to the stakes, not
systematically maximal. Risk-free email sorting needs only an occasional glance; a
quote draft is reread before sending; nothing committing goes out **without
validation.** The module M1 rule stays the thread: what's checkable and matters
must be checked. An automation doesn't remove responsibility, it concentrates it
into a final look.
</Attention>

## Knowing when to unplug

<Idee>
Not every automation deserves to survive. If one asks for more corrections than it
saves, if the task has changed, or if you no longer use it: **unplug it without
regret.** Keeping a useless automation on principle is complexity for nothing.
Measuring also serves to know what to stop.
</Idee>

## The system, not the gadgets

By the end of this module, you hold a coherent little system: spot the repetitive
(lesson 1), hand it to a framed assistant (lesson 2), bring it closer to your
tools (lesson 3), and keep only what really saves time (this lesson). It's not a
collection of tricks, it's a **method** — the same clear-eyed mindset as since
module M1, applied to productivity.

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Take the automation (or assistant) you set up in this module. Measure it honestly
over **3 real cases**:

1. Time **before** (by hand).
2. Time **after**, **checking included.**
3. The **net gain** — and the question: *is it worth it?*

Then decide: you **keep** it (and note the right safeguard), you **improve** it (a
rule to add), or you **unplug** it. That's how you build real productivity, not
the for-show kind.
</Exercice>

## In short

- The real gain = time of the task **minus** setup **minus** checking. Aim for
  **net time freed.**
- You **measure** over a few real cases rather than trusting an impression.
- The checking **safeguard** is **proportionate to the stakes**; nothing
  committing goes out without validation.
- You **unplug without regret** what doesn't pay off — and the whole forms a
  **method**, not gadgets.

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min module synthesis (spot → assistant → connect →
measure) · 4 min on measuring the net gain, with a live calculation · 3 min on the
proportionate safeguard · 2 min on knowing when to unplug · 4 min measuring
exercise.

**Landing the module.** Have a participant calculate aloud the net gain of THEIR
automation (before / after, checking included). The concrete figure — sometimes
disappointing, sometimes stunning — is the best lesson: you judge on net time, not
on the "wow" effect.

**Tying it to the rest of the programme.** This is where the M1 mindset closes at
the scale of productivity: automate without giving up control. Naming this thread
helps the group see the coherence of the whole journey.

**Frequent questions**
- *"How long to recoup the setup?"* → if the task is frequent (the right criterion
  from lesson 1), payback is fast, often a few days. On a rare task, the
  automation never pays off — which is why you don't automate it.
- *"Do I really have to measure every time?"* → a quick measure at the start is
  enough to decide keep/unplug. After that, you only revisit if a doubt appears.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't let the group pile up automations for the technical
fun of it. The lesson is a safeguard: fewer automations, but measured and
reliable, beat an unmanageable stack.
</Formateur>

"Automation" sounds technical, almost intimidating. Yet the hardest part isn't
setting up an automation: it's **choosing well what to automate.** A poorly chosen
automated task wastes time and introduces errors; a well-chosen one works for you,
quietly. So this module begins with spotting.

## The key idea

<Idee>
You automate what's **repetitive, frequent and predictable** — tasks that follow
roughly the same rules every time. You keep in hand what needs **judgement,
relationship or responsibility.** The good candidate is boring and recurring; the
bad candidate is rare, sensitive or irreversible.
</Idee>

## The three criteria of a good task

A task is worth automating when it ticks all three:

- **Repetitive** — you do it the same way every time.
- **Frequent** — it comes up often (even a small gain multiplies).
- **Low-stakes** — an error is recoverable without harm.

<Exemple>
- **Good candidates**: sorting incoming emails by type, turning notes into a
  report, preparing a standard reply to a frequent question, reformatting a list,
  drafting a quote from standard elements.
- **Bad candidates**: deciding to hire, replying to an angry client, setting a
  strategic price, signing a document. Here, the time "saved" is never worth the
  risk.
</Exemple>

## Keep the human where it counts

<Attention>
The temptation, once you've tasted automation, is to want to automate everything
— including judgement. That's a mistake. The more a task **commits** you (a
decision, a relationship, a legal or financial responsibility), the more it must
stay in human hands. Automating isn't delegating your discernment: it's getting
rid of the repetitive **so that** you can focus on what matters.
</Attention>

## Taking stock of your week

The best way to spot your candidates: watch your own daily work. For a few days,
note the tasks that give you the feeling of "doing the same thing over and over."
Those are your targets.

<Idee>
For each recurring task, ask yourself two questions: *"Do I do this the same way
every time?"* and *"Is it serious if the result needs correcting?"* If it's
**yes** to the first and **no** to the second, you've got an excellent automation
candidate.
</Idee>

## Prioritise: start with the most rewarding

You'll have several candidates. Start with the one that combines **high
frequency** and **low stakes**: it pays off fastest, with the least risk. Then you
expand, one task at a time — as with adopting AI in module M1.

## Your turn

<Exercice>
List **5 tasks** you repeated this week. For each, note two things: its
**frequency** (rare / weekly / daily) and the **stakes of an error** (low /
medium / high).

Circle the one that's the **most frequent** AND the **lowest-stakes**. That's your
first target: the next lessons will show you how to hand it to AI. Conversely, set
aside the high-stakes ones: they'll stay yours.
</Exercice>

## In short

- Automate the **repetitive + frequent + low-stakes**; keep the **judgement,
  relationship, responsibility.**
- The good candidate is **boring and recurring**; the bad one is **rare, sensitive
  or irreversible.**
- Spot your targets by **watching your own week.**
- **Prioritise** by frequency × low stakes, and start with just one.

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min defusing fear ("automate" scares for nothing) ·
4 min on the three criteria with good/bad candidates · 3 min on "keep the human" ·
2 min on prioritising · 4 min live stock-taking.

**Collective stock-taking exercise.** Have each person say aloud one "always the
same" task from their week. Sort good/bad candidate together on screen. The group
quickly grasps the criterion — and notices its best targets are the chores it
hates, which is motivating.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Will AI replace me if I automate my work?"* → you automate *tasks*, not
  *jobs*; the freed-up time goes to what has value (relationship, judgement).
  Return to the M1 mindset.
- *"What if the task sometimes changes?"* → if it changes often, it's a bad
  candidate (not predictable enough). An occasional exception is handled by hand.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't over-value spectacular automations. A small, boring
but daily automation pays off more, and more reliably, than an impressive setup
used once a month.
</Formateur>

Until now, you've worked by copy-pasting between AI and your documents. The next
step is to **bring AI closer to your tools** to cut down those round trips. Good
news: in the vast majority of cases, **you won't have to code anything.** Let's
look at the levels, from simplest to most advanced.

## The key idea

<Idee>
"Connecting AI to your tools" covers several levels, and the most useful is often
the simplest: AI is **already built into** many of the programs you use (email,
word processor, spreadsheet, management tools). Start there before imagining
complicated setups.
</Idee>

## Level 1: the AI already in your software

More and more everyday tools include an AI assistant directly: drafting a reply in
your email, summarising a document, creating a table, rephrasing a note. It's the
most accessible level — nothing to install, nothing to connect.

<Exemple>
*"Summarise this email thread and suggest a reply"* right inside your inbox;
*"format this table and calculate the totals"* in your spreadsheet; *"rephrase
this paragraph"* in your word processor. You use AI without ever leaving the
tool. Look for the "assistant", "AI" button or the sparkle ✦ in the software you
already have.
</Exemple>

## Level 2: automate between two tools (without coding)

The next level links **two applications**: "when a new message arrives, file it";
"when a form is filled in, prepare a draft reply." So-called **no-code** tools (no
programming) let you set up these chains by assembling blocks, like bricks.

<Idee>
The principle is always the same: a **trigger** ("when this happens"), an
**action** ("do that"), sometimes an AI step in the middle ("summarise",
"classify", "draft"). You describe the logic; the tool runs it. It's assembly,
not development.
</Idee>

## The data question: don't skip it

<Attention>
As soon as you connect tools, **data flows** — sometimes client, financial or
personal data. Before connecting anything, ask yourself: *what information will I
pass through, and where does it go?* Connect the minimum necessary, keep a **human
validation** on what goes out (an automatically sent email is still your
responsibility), and refer to module M5 for the rules (GDPR, AI Act). A handy
automation must never become a data leak.
</Attention>

## Level 3: the bespoke (and when to stop)

There are more advanced connections (developed specifically for your business).
That's a real project, with a cost and a provider. **Never start there.** The
overwhelming majority of gains are at levels 1 and 2, with no budget or
technician. Bespoke is only justified when a precise, frequent and quantified
need genuinely calls for it.

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Two paths, choose by your comfort level:

1. **Level 1 (everyone)**: open a piece of software you use every day and **look
   for its AI feature** (assistant button, ✦, "AI" menu). Have it do a small real
   task. Note whether it saves you a copy-paste.
2. **Level 2 (the curious)**: identify a "when X, then Y" chain from your daily
   work and describe it in one sentence (trigger → action). You don't have to
   build it today: spotting the logic is already the essential.

For each path, ask yourself **what data** would be involved.
</Exercice>

## In short

- Simplest first: the AI **already built into** your software (level 1).
- Level 2 links two tools with **no-code** tools: trigger → (AI step) → action,
  **without coding.**
- Always think about the **data flowing** and keep a **human validation**
  (details in M5).
- **Bespoke** (level 3) is a project: go there only last, on a quantified need.

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min framing (cut down copy-pasting) · 4 min level 1
with a demo in a common tool · 4 min level 2 (trigger → action, a telling
example) · 3 min on data · 2 min level 3 + launching the exercise.

**Stay concrete, avoid the complex no-code demo.** Showing level 1 live (AI in
the inbox or spreadsheet) marks the group. For level 2, describing the logic on
the board is enough; don't launch into building a no-code scenario in the
session, you'd lose half the room.

**Adapt to the group's tools.** Ask beforehand which tools they use (office
suite, email, management). Showing the AI feature of THEIR tools is far more
telling than a generic demo.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Is no-code free?"* → a free tier is often enough to start; beyond that, a
  modest subscription. To assess case by case, no pressure.
- *"Is it risky to connect everything?"* → yes if you connect broadly without
  thinking about the data. Hence the rule: minimum necessary + human validation.
  That's the bridge to M5.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't sell total automation ("the machine does
everything"). The module's message stays the M1 mindset: automate the
repetitive, keep control of what goes out and of the data.
</Formateur>

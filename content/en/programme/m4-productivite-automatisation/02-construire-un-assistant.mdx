You've spotted a task to automate (lesson 1). The next step isn't technical: it's
about **fixing the right request once and for all**, so you no longer have to
re-explain everything each time. It's the direct extension of the templates from
module M2 — more structured and more powerful.

## The key idea

<Idee>
An "assistant", here, isn't an autonomous robot: it's a **set of stable
instructions** you give once and reuse afterward. You define its **role**, the
**context** of your trade, the **rules** to always respect and the **format**
expected. Then you just submit the day's case. The bulk of the framing work is
done once.
</Idee>

Most AI tools let you save this kind of assistant (under various names: "custom
instructions", "GPTs", "projects"…). But even without a dedicated feature, just
keep your instructions in a document and paste them at the top of each request.

## The four bricks of an assistant

<Exemple>
A "reply to quote requests" assistant could fit in these instructions, kept once
and for all:

> **Role:** you reply to quote requests for my joinery business.
> **Context:** private clients, warm and professional tone, I do bespoke
> woodwork in the Lyon region.
> **Rules:** always thank them for the request, announce a reply within 48h,
> never give a firm price (offer a meeting), stay under 8 lines.
> **Format:** an email ready to send, subject line included.

In use, you add only the client's message. The assistant already knows the rest.
</Exemple>

## Role, context, rules, format

- **Role** — what the assistant does, in one sentence.
- **Context** — what never changes: your trade, your clientele, your tone.
- **Rules** — the instructions to always respect (and the don'ts).
- **Format** — the exact shape of the expected output.

The more precise these four bricks, the less you'll have to correct afterward.

## Test before relying

<Attention>
An assistant isn't reliable because it's written: it's reliable because it's been
**tested.** Before using it for real, run **several varied real cases** through
it, including slightly tricky ones. You'll quickly see where its instructions are
too vague or produce an off result — and you fix the instructions, not each
result. A poorly tested assistant repeats its error at scale.
</Attention>

## Making it evolve

<Idee>
Like a template (module M2), an assistant **improves through use.** Each time a
result forces a recurring correction, add the matching rule to its instructions.
Over the weeks, it needs fewer and fewer fixes — the initial effort pays off many
times over.
</Idee>

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Take the target task from lesson 1. Write its assistant with the **four bricks**:
role, context, rules, format. Be precise about the rules (what it must always do,
what it must never do).

Then **test it on 3 different real cases.** At each slip, don't fix the result:
fix the **instruction.** After three tries, you'll have an assistant you can reuse
dozens of times.
</Exercice>

## In short

- An assistant = **stable, reusable instructions**, not an autonomous robot.
- Four bricks: **role, context, rules, format.**
- You **test it on several real cases** and fix the **instructions**, not the
  results.
- It **evolves through use**, like a template — the framing effort is done once.

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min linking to the M2 templates · 5 min on the four
bricks, with an assistant built live · 4 min on multi-case testing · 2 min on
making it evolve · 2 min launching the exercise.

**Live building.** Take a participant's task and write its assistant on screen,
brick by brick, then test it on two cases. The "aha" comes at the test: a tricky
case reveals a missing rule, added live. That's the pedagogical core of the
lesson.

**Intermediate level.** This is the programme's first lesson marked
"intermediate": some participants will be comfortable, others less. Stress that
you **reuse everything** seen before (context/goal/format from M1, templates from
M2) — nothing new, just assembled.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Do I need a paid tool to save an assistant?"* → no: a document with the
  instructions to copy-paste is enough. Dedicated features (GPTs, projects) are a
  convenience, not a necessity.
- *"Can my assistant act on its own (send the email)?"* → that touches on
  connecting to tools (lesson 3); and even then, we keep a human validation on
  what goes out. Patience.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't aim for the "perfect" assistant on the first go. The
goal is to learn the write → test → fix loop. An imperfect but tested assistant
beats an ambitious one that's never been tried.
</Formateur>

A group is never homogeneous: some already handle AI, others fear "pressing the
wrong button"; some are enthusiastic, others wary, even hostile. Managing this
diversity well is often what makes the difference between an adequate session and
one that transforms.

## The key idea

<Idee>
Your group will always have **very different levels and comfort zones.** Your job
isn't to bring everyone to the same level, but to make sure **each person
progresses from their own** and leaves confident. You pull the group up without
leaving anyone on the sidelines.
</Idee>

## Coping with level gaps

A few simple tactics:

- **Set the pace at the middle**, not at the fastest.
- **Enlist the most comfortable**: have them help their neighbour, it values them
  and eases the facilitation.
- **Reassure the most hesitant** privately during exercises, without exposing
  them.

<Exemple>
During an exercise, one participant has already finished while another doesn't
dare start. Pair the first as a "buddy" with the second. The fast one isn't bored,
the hesitant one is unblocked by a peer (often less intimidating than the
trainer), and you gain calm.
</Exemple>

## Welcoming fears

<Attention>
Reluctance — fear of being replaced, mistrust, feeling "too old for this" — is
**legitimate** and must be welcomed, never brushed aside or mocked. A minimised
fear turns into shutdown for the rest of the session. Listen, validate the
emotion, then bring it back to the programme's posture: AI takes tasks, the human
keeps the judgement and the value. You defuse without denying.
</Attention>

## Answering difficult questions

<Idee>
You'll get sharp questions or ones with no certain answer (compliance, job
replacement, reliability). The golden rule echoes the trainer's posture:
**honesty.** Better "good question, I'm not sure, here's where to find a reliable
answer" than a confident bluff. On sensitive topics (GDPR, AI Act), point to
module M5 and official sources rather than settling it.
</Idee>

## Handling special cases

The vocal sceptic: give them a real case of their own, the result speaks better
than you. The talker who monopolises: "great point, let's pick it up at the
break", and hand the floor back. The silent one: gently draw them in on a point
where they're comfortable. The goal is that **the group stays a safe space** where
people dare to try and to get it wrong.

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Anticipate **three tricky group situations** you dread (a sceptic, a marked level
gap, a fear voiced aloud). For each, prepare **one welcoming sentence** and **one
concrete action** (buddy pairing, real case, pointing to a source).

Having these answers ready will keep you from being caught off guard — and keep
you in the facilitator's posture, even under pressure.
</Exercice>

## In short

- The group is always **mixed**: have each person progress from their level,
  without leaving anyone behind.
- You **enlist the most comfortable** (buddy pairings) and reassure the hesitant.
- You **welcome fears** without brushing them aside, then bring it back to the
  posture.
- On hard questions: **honesty** and pointing to reliable sources (M5 for
  compliance).

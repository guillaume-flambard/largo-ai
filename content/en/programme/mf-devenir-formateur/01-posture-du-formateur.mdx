Welcome to the trainer layer. If you're reading this, you want to pass it on in
turn. Good news: training people in AI doesn't require being the greatest expert
in the room. It requires a **posture** — and that, more than technique, makes good
sessions.

## The key idea

<Idee>
The AI trainer isn't an **all-knowing expert**, but a **facilitator.** Your role
isn't to dazzle with your mastery, but to have people **practise**, reassure them,
and give the right reflexes. A participant who has completed a real task and
understood the posture has learned more than one impressed by your demos.
</Idee>

## You don't have to know everything

<Attention>
AI moves fast, no one masters it all — and wanting to seem infallible is the best
way to trap yourself. Own your limits: faced with a question you don't know the
answer to, say so, and **search with the group.** Better still: make it a living
demonstration of the programme's posture ("we don't know, so we check at the
source"). Your honesty builds trust; false omniscience would destroy it at the
first slip.
</Attention>

## Pass on the posture before the tools

The heart of the Largo programme isn't a list of tools, it's a **posture**: use AI
then verify, stay in control, remain responsible. That's what you pass on first.
The tools will change; the posture stays valid.

<Exemple>
A participant asks "what's the best tool?". The expert's answer would be a
technical comparison. The **trainer's** answer is: "the one you'll actually use —
and whatever the tool, the reflex stays the same: give context, then check what
matters." You bring it back to the posture rather than the technique.
</Exemple>

## Embody what you teach

<Idee>
Your best teaching is example. If you show AI getting it wrong and you **verify in
front of the group**, you teach the posture through action. If you acknowledge one
of your own prompt mistakes and fix it, you show it's normal. The credible trainer
isn't the one who never errs: it's the one who shows what to do when it happens.
</Idee>

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Prepare your **answer to three trap questions** participants might ask and that
you're unsure how to answer ("is this tool GDPR-compliant?", "will AI wipe out my
job?", "what's the best model?").

For each, practise a **facilitator's** answer: own the uncertainty, bring it back
to the posture, or show how to find the reliable answer. You'll see: not knowing
becomes a teaching asset.
</Exercice>

## In short

- The AI trainer is a **facilitator**, not an all-knowing expert: you have people
  **practise.**
- You **own not knowing everything**; an unanswered question becomes a demo of the
  posture (check at the source).
- You pass on **the posture before the tools** (use then verify, stay in control).
- You **embody** what you teach: showing the error and the fix beats displayed
  perfection.

A good session isn't recognised by the number of slides, but by what the
participants **did.** To run one is to orchestrate an alternation between
understanding, seeing and practising — while keeping the group's energy. Here's
how.

## The key idea

<Idee>
The rhythm of a lively session alternates three short beats: **an idea** (brief
explanation), **a demo** (you show), **practice** (they do). You repeat this cycle
rather than explaining everything in one block before letting the group loose.
Practice isn't the cherry on top: it's the heart of learning.
</Idee>

## Have people practise, not just demonstrate

<Attention>
The classic trap of the AI trainer is the **chain of demonstrations**: you string
feats together, the group is impressed… and leaves unable to do anything. The
"wow" effect isn't learning. Always prefer **a real exercise** to one more
spectacular demo. Better a participant who succeeded at an email on THEIR case
than ten admired demos.
</Attention>

## Lean on real cases

The fuel of a session is the participants' own cases. Ask for them early and use
them:

<Exemple>
Rather than your prepared example, launch: "Who has a difficult email to write
right now?". Handle it live, with the group. The participant leaves with a useful
deliverable, and the others see the method on a credible case, not a theoretical
one. Every Largo module lends itself to this (an email for M2, a post for M3, a
task to automate for M4…).
</Exemple>

## Use the "For the trainer" zones

<Idee>
Each lesson of modules M1 to M5 contains a **"For the trainer"** zone: a timed
flow, the demo that lands, the frequent questions and the facilitation pitfalls.
It's your preparation cheat sheet — not a text to read to the group. Skim it
before each session: it gives you the tempo and the tried-and-tested key moments.
</Idee>

## Manage time and energy

A few simple markers: announce the plan at the start, keep practice slots
**short** (5-10 min) rather than one big final exercise, and watch the energy — one
demo too many after lunch sends people to sleep, an exercise wakes them up. Better
to cover less but have people practise than to skim everything without anchoring
anything.

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Take **one lesson** of a Largo module (e.g. M2 lesson 1). From its "For the
trainer" zone, write your **15-minute flow**: where you place the idea, the demo,
and above all the moment of **practice on a real case**.

Then identify the **one** moment you won't sacrifice if time runs short. (Hint:
it'll almost always be the practice.)
</Exercice>

## In short

- Lively rhythm = short **idea → demo → practice** cycles, repeated.
- You **have people practise** on real cases; you avoid the chain of
  demonstrations.
- The **participants' cases** are the best fuel for the session.
- The **"For the trainer"** zones give flow, demos and pitfalls — your prep cheat
  sheet.

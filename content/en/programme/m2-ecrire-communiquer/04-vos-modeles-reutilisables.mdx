You've learned to get good emails, to rephrase, to write reports. The last step
of the module, and perhaps the most rewarding: **don't start from scratch every
time.** Your best requests can become templates — a small capital that works for
you.

## The key idea

<Idee>
A template isn't a final text you send as is: it's a **request that worked
well**, cleaned up and gapped in the right places to be reused. The first time,
you search for the right wording; after that, you just fill in the gaps. That's
what turns "I reinvent every time" into "I have my tools that run."
</Idee>

## From one-off request to template

The recipe is simple. When a request gave you an excellent result, instead of
discarding it:

1. **Keep it** (a notes file, a shared document, whatever).
2. **Spot what changes** from one time to the next — a name, a date, a
   case-specific detail — and replace it with a clear marker, in `[brackets]`.
3. **Keep what doesn't change**: your trade context, the tone, the structure.

<Exemple>
A one-off request that worked:

> *"I'm a plumber. Reply to this client asking for a quote to replace a water
> heater, clear and reassuring tone: lead time under 1 week, free callout, quote
> valid for 30 days."*

Turned into a template:

> *"I'm a plumber. Reply to a client asking for a quote for `[type of work]`,
> clear and reassuring tone: lead time `[lead time]`, `[conditions]`, quote valid
> for 30 days."*

The next quote request takes ten seconds.
</Exemple>

## A library you can actually find

<Attention>
A template you can't find doesn't exist. No need to build a factory: a simple
document with clear headings ("Quote", "Follow-up", "Site report") is plenty at
first. The criterion isn't quantity, but **how fast you can lay hands on it** the
moment you need it. Five templates you use beat thirty you've forgotten.
</Attention>

## Keeping your templates alive

<Idee>
A template is never finished. Each time you use it and a detail bothers you, fix
it **in the template**, not just in today's email. Over the weeks, your templates
refine themselves and get more and more on point — it's use that perfects them,
not a big upfront effort.
</Idee>

And of course, the module M1 habit still holds: a template speeds up the
**form**, it doesn't excuse you from **checking the facts** you slot into it
(amounts, lead times, dates).

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Think back to **one request** from the previous lessons that gave you a good
result (an email, a standard report). Turn it into a template:

1. Clean it up and replace with `[brackets]` everything that changes from case to
   case.
2. File it in a document, under a clear heading.
3. Test it **right away** on a different real case: fill the gaps, run it, adjust
   the template if needed.

You've just created your first reusable tool. Aim for two or three for your most
frequent tasks: that's where the time saved becomes visible.
</Exercice>

## In short

- A template = a **good request**, cleaned up and **gapped** (`[brackets]`) where
  things change.
- A **simple library** (clear headings, quickly found) beats a complicated
  system.
- Templates are **kept alive**: you fix them through use, they refine themselves.
- They speed up the **form**; you **always check the facts** (M1 habit).

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min framing (don't start from scratch) · 4 min on
the request → template transformation with the gapped example live · 3 min on the
library · 2 min on keeping alive · 4 min exercise.

**Landing the module.** This is M2's closing lesson: everyone should leave with
**at least one template** created during the session, not just the idea. Have the
template built live on a real task from each participant. It's the M2 equivalent
of the concrete end-of-M1 commitment.

**Connection to other modules.** The "keep what works" move sets up module M4
(productivity & automation): a well-made template is the first step toward
automation. Flag it without getting into the subject.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Where do I actually store my templates?"* → where you'll already go looking
  for them: a shared doc, a notepad, your email's templates. The best location is
  the one you open without thinking.
- *"How many templates do I need?"* → start with your 2-3 most frequent tasks.
  The library grows on its own with use.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't send the group off building a big exhaustive library.
Two living templates beat twenty dead ones. Quantity will come from use, not from
a cataloguing session.
</Formateur>

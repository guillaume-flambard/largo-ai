Email is probably where AI will save you time fastest: it's frequent, it's
writing, and the stakes stay moderate. But an email isn't just text — it's a
relationship. Let's see how AI helps you write fast **without** sounding like a
robot.

## The key idea

<Idee>
For an email, three details are enough to go from a lukewarm result to the right
one: **who** you're writing to (and what your relationship is), **what you want
to achieve**, and **the tone** you want. It's the email version of the
context/goal/format habit from module M1.
</Idee>

## The everyday email: fast and good

For emails with no particular difficulty — confirming a meeting, passing on
information, answering a simple request — AI is unbeatable on speed. Give it the
essentials in bulk, it shapes them:

<Exemple>
*"Reply to this client asking about our lead times. Tell them: 2 to 3 weeks
depending on workload, quote valid for 30 days, we can set up a call this week.
Clear and cordial tone, short."* → a clean email, ready to reread and send.
</Exemple>

## The delicate email: where AI really helps

It's on difficult messages that AI is most useful — not by deciding for you, but
by helping you **find the right tone** and **step back.**

- **A follow-up** that's neither sappy nor aggressive.
- **A refusal** that preserves the relationship.
- **An apology** that's sincere without grovelling.
- **A message written hot**, which you ask it to "make calmer" before sending.

<Exemple>
You're annoyed by a late supplier. Write your message as it comes, even curt,
then: *"Rephrase this message so it stays firm on substance but courteous in
form, without aggression."* The AI defuses the tension while keeping your
demand. You reread, and you're the one who sends.
</Exemple>

## Keeping your voice

<Attention>
The risk of AI email is **uniformity**: that smooth, slightly corporate tone,
recognisable a mile off ("I'm just reaching out to follow up…"). Your recipients
spot it, and it cools things down. The fix is simple: **give an example of your
own emails** and ask it to write "in this spirit." Your style beats the default
one.
</Attention>

A few useful settings to break the generic feel: ask for a **shorter** email
(AI is wordy), **drop the stock phrases**, and **keep your verbal tics** if you
have likeable ones.

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Take a delicate email you keep putting off (a follow-up that's dragging, a
refusal to phrase). First write the gist in two raw lines, unpolished. Then ask
the AI: **recipient + intent + tone.** Make one tweak ("shorter", "less
formal").

Reread asking yourself one question: *does this sound like me?* If not, paste one
of your real emails as a model and run it again. The email you were putting off
is probably ready.
</Exercice>

## In short

- For an email: specify **recipient, intent, tone.**
- AI shines on **delicate messages** — follow-up, refusal, apology, hot message —
  by helping with tone and perspective, not with the decision.
- **Keep your voice**: an example of your emails beats the standard tone.
- You **reread and you send**: responsibility stays yours (M1 habit).

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min framing (email = relationship, not just text) ·
3 min on the everyday email · 5 min on the delicate email with a live "hot
message" rephrasing · 3 min on keeping your voice · 2 min launching the exercise.

**The strong moment.** Do the "hot message" exercise live: ask the group for a
recent professional annoyance, type the curt message, then rephrase on screen.
The "oh yes, that's better" effect is immediate and telling.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Will people tell it was written by an AI?"* → only if you keep the standard
  tone. Hence the importance of the personal model. A short, natural email
  doesn't give itself away.
- *"What about emails in English / another language?"* → an excellent use, but
  check the tone (politeness codes differ); we come back to it in lesson 2
  (translating).

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't hand out a ready-made "perfect email template." The
goal is the habit (recipient/intent/tone + keep your voice), not a single
template that would make everyone sound the same.
</Formateur>

The report is a classic chore: you have scattered notes, sometimes just
sentence fragments, and you need to turn them into a readable document. This is
exactly the kind of transformation AI excels at — as long as you keep an eye on
what it **adds** to make things look nice.

## The key idea

<Idee>
From your notes, AI can **structure** (group, prioritise, format) far faster than
you. But it also tends to **smooth over the gaps**: where your notes are thin, it
fills in with the plausible. The rule: it organises your material, it must not
invent any.
</Idee>

## Give material, not a miracle

The richer your notes, the better the report. Throw in everything you have — even
in telegraphic style, even misspelled — and state the structure you want:

<Exemple>
*"Here are my scattered meeting notes [paste]. Write a clear report organised in
three parts: 1) decisions made, 2) actions to take with who and by when, 3) open
points. Stay factual, add nothing that isn't in my notes."*

The instruction "add nothing that isn't in my notes" is the most important: it
reins in the tendency to embroider.
</Exemple>

## Structure for action, not for archiving

A good report isn't a verbatim. Ask for a layout **geared toward what comes
next**:

- **Decisions** made (the list, not the debate).
- **Actions**: who does what, by when.
- **Open**: what's left to settle.

It's this shape that makes the document useful a week later, when no one
remembers the details.

## The trap: plausible invention

<Attention>
From incomplete notes, the AI can **fabricate** a decision, assign an action to
the wrong person, or pin down a date you hadn't noted — all while looking
perfectly credible. In a report, these inventions are dangerous: people later
refer to them as truth. **Reread against your notes**, prioritising the actions,
names and dates.
</Attention>

## A useful variant: prepare, not just record

<Idee>
AI doesn't only help **after** the meeting. **Before**, ask it for an agenda or a
list of questions from the objective ("we need to decide X"). **During**, keep
your raw notes without worrying about form: you know it'll clean them up
afterward. The report starts taking shape before the meeting even happens.
</Idee>

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Take the notes from your last meeting (or a call) — even incomplete. Ask the AI
for a report structured into **decisions / actions / open**, with the
instruction *"add nothing that isn't in my notes."*

Then reread with your notes alongside and hunt down **one invention**: an action
wrongly assigned, a date appearing out of nowhere, a decision a touch too clean.
You'll quickly see where the AI "helped" a little too much.
</Exercice>

## In short

- AI **structures** your notes fast; it must not **invent** what they don't say.
- The key instruction: *"add nothing that isn't in my notes."*
- Aim for a report **geared toward action**: decisions, actions (who/what/when),
  open points.
- **Reread against your notes** — actions, names, dates first (M1 habit).

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min framing (the report chore) · 4 min on giving
material + the decisions/actions/open structure · 4 min on the invention trap,
with a real case · 5 min exercise on a participant's notes.

**A telling demonstration.** Bring deliberately thin notes and have the report
produced live: point out together a "made-up" sentence that nothing in the notes
justified. It turns the abstract warning into a concrete rereading habit.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Can it transcribe an audio recording?"* → depending on the tool, yes; but
  that's a more advanced use (and sensitive for confidentiality — M5). Here we
  start from written notes.
- *"Do I have to reread everything every time?"* → target the risk: actions,
  names, figures, dates. The connecting prose can be trusted.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't sell the "perfect automatic report." The message is
the opposite: AI structures so well that you lower your guard — which is exactly
when you must reread against your notes.
</Formateur>

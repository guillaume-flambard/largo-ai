Rephrase, summarise, translate: these are the three moves where AI is truly at
home, because they require **no new information** — just reworking material you
give it. It's the safest and most rewarding ground. Let's see how to steer each
one well.

## The key idea

<Idee>
These three moves start from a text **you provide**: the AI isn't at risk of
inventing a fact, it transforms what's there. The risk shifts to **tone** and
**faithfulness to meaning.** Your job is no longer to check facts, but to check
that nothing important was lost or distorted.
</Idee>

## Rephrase: same substance, different form

Rephrasing means keeping the content while changing the tone, the register or the
target audience. Always say **for whom** and **to what end**:

<Exemple>
- *"Rephrase this technical paragraph for a non-specialist client, simple and
  reassuring."*
- *"Make this message more direct, without the bureaucratic feel."*
- *"Same content, but in three short sentences for a text message."*
</Exemple>

## Summarise: set the length and the sorting

A summary with no brief gives a random result. Two details change everything:
**what length**, and **what you want out of it** (the decisions? the figures? the
next actions?).

<Exemple>
*"Summarise this meeting report in 5 bullets: only the decisions made and who
does what. Leave out the discussions."* → an action-oriented summary, not a
lukewarm digest of everything.
</Exemple>

## Translate: beware the tone, not the word

AI translates remarkably well. The trap isn't everyday vocabulary, it's what
doesn't translate word for word:

<Attention>
Two points of vigilance in translation: the **register of politeness** (a
"normal" email in one language can sound curt or too familiar in another —
forms of address and opening phrases differ), and the **trade terms or proper
nouns** (a poorly rendered technical word discredits the whole message). For a
text that matters, give the context: *"Translate this professional email into
English for a British client, courteous and professional tone."*
</Attention>

Tip: to check a translation without speaking the language, ask the AI to
**translate its result back** into your language. If the meaning comes back
intact, that's a good sign (without being an absolute guarantee).

## One text, three uses

<Idee>
The real gain appears when you **chain** the moves on the same material: a raw
report you have **summarised** for management, **rephrased** as a team message,
then **translated** for a foreign partner. One source, three tailored outputs, in
a few minutes.
</Idee>

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Take a text you have at hand (a report, a note, an email you received). Put it
through the **three moves**:

1. **Summarise it** by setting a length ("4 bullets") and a sorting ("only the
   actions").
2. **Rephrase** the summary for a specific audience (your team, a client).
3. **Translate** the result into a language useful to your work, specifying the
   tone.

Check at each step that no important information dropped out. You now hold a mini
workshop reusable on any document.
</Exercice>

## In short

- Rephrase / summarise / translate start from a text **you give**: little risk of
  invention, vigilance on **tone** and **meaning.**
- To **summarise**, set **length + sorting**; to **rephrase**, state **audience +
  goal.**
- In **translation**, watch the **register of politeness** and the **trade
  terms**; back-translation helps you check.
- The biggest gain: **chain** the three moves on a single source.

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 1 min framing (three moves "without invention") ·
3 min rephrasing · 3 min summarising (stress the sorting) · 4 min translating
(tone, back-translation) · 4 min chained exercise.

**A striking demo.** Take a text from the group and do the three moves in a row
on screen: summary → rephrasing → translation. Seeing one source yield three
deliverables in two minutes locks in the "reusable workshop" idea.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Can whole documents be translated?"* → yes, but reread the sensitive
  passages; and beware of confidential data (M1 habit, details in M5).
- *"Is the summary reliable?"* → on a provided text, yes for the general meaning;
  check that no important nuance was flattened. A summary is a choice of what to
  keep.

**Pitfall to avoid:** don't dwell on feats of literary translation. The goal is
everyday professional use (emails, notes, docs), where tone matters more than
elegance.
</Formateur>

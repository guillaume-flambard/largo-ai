# Measuring ROI on the business side

The ROI of AI-first training is not measured the same way depending on whether you are the CTO (velocity, code quality, debt) or the CEO/CIO (time-to-market, operational cost, regulatory risk). This module addresses both — and the conversation between the two.

## The calculation that leadership understands

Leadership is not insensitive to technical arguments. They are insensitive to arguments that do not quantify. The CTO must be able to translate.

**On the gains side:**

- **Developer velocity**: if a team of 5 devs saves an average of 30% of time on CRUD and test tasks (conservative based on field measurements), that is the equivalent of 1.5 additional developers without hiring. At the average cost of a senior developer (€60–80k fully loaded), the value created is measurable.

- **Time-to-ship**: shortening the spec-to-production cycle by 30% means delivering more features per quarter, iterating faster on the product, and reducing the time between investment and revenue.

- **Technical debt reduction**: an unguided AI-first workflow generates debt (unreviewed code, superficial tests). An AI-first workflow with guardrails reduces debt — fewer "debt sprints" at the end of the roadmap.

<Idee>
For leadership, the most effective ROI framing is one that speaks to risks avoided, not just gains. "Training your teams in August 2026 costs X. Not training them and falling under an AI Act investigation could cost up to €15M or 3% of turnover." The defensive frame often unlocks the decision faster than the performance frame.
</Idee>

**On the hidden costs of non-adoption:**

- **Post-merge bugs from unreviewed AI code**: each production bug costs on average 6 to 10× the time it would have taken to fix before the merge. Without training, the error-to-generated-code ratio is high.

- **Shadow AI and GDPR risks**: a customer data leak via an unvalidated AI tool can trigger a CNIL investigation. A data breach notification costs in reputation, legal resources, and potential GDPR fines (up to 4% of global turnover).

- **AI Act non-compliance**: for organisations that develop or deploy high-risk systems (HR, scoring, evaluation), sanctions can reach €15M or 3% of global annual turnover.

## Building the ROI table for leadership

A simple, honest format — no fantasy projections, only what is measurable:

<Exemple>
**AI-first training ROI table — team of 6 developers**

| | Estimated amount |
|---|---|
| **Training cost** | On quote (contact us) |
| **Velocity gain (conservative: +25%)** | ~1.5 FTE saved over 12 months |
| **Cost of FTE avoided (senior, fully loaded)** | ~€90,000 |
| **Post-merge bug reduction (estimated)** | ~15 developer days recovered |
| **Unaddressed Shadow AI cost (GDPR risk)** | Variable — potentially €10,000+ per incident |
| **AI Act risk (if high-risk system)** | Up to €15M (documented non-compliance) |
| **Payback horizon** | 3 to 6 months depending on team velocity |

*These estimates are based on typical field measurements — your context may differ. Actual time is measured from the first post-training sprint.*
</Exemple>

## What leadership must decide

Training is an investment — it only pays off if teams genuinely adopt the workflow. Leadership must make two commitments for the ROI to materialise:

1. **Provide the tools**: without validated AI tool licences, developers return to Shadow AI after training. The investment is wasted.

2. **Protect adoption time**: during the first two weeks post-training, velocity drops (ramp-up). If pressure is immediately at maximum, teams abandon the new workflow. Schedule training outside critical delivery peaks.

<Attention>
Do not present your leadership with a 300% ROI in 3 months. Those projections exist in marketing studies — they do not correspond to field measurements on real teams. An honest case with conservative, measurable figures is more credible, and survives confrontation with post-training reality.
</Attention>

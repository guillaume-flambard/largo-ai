In the previous lesson, we saw that AI is good at writing — provided you give it
context. This lesson goes further: **how to phrase a request so the result is
useful from the very first draft**, and what to do when it isn't. Good news:
there's no magic formula to memorise, just three simple habits.

## The key idea

<Idee>
The quality of an answer depends mostly on the quality of the request. A good
instruction isn't a *long* or *technical* one: it's an instruction that says
**who** you're talking to (context), **what you want** (goal) and **in what
shape** (format). The rest is noise.
</Idee>

Talking to AI is like briefing a capable intern who knows nothing about your
situation: they'll do good work if you give them enough to understand. Too
vague, and they guess; and they often guess wrong.

## The three ingredients of a good request

1. **Context** — who you are, who the text is for, in what situation. *"I run an
   accounting firm, writing to a client annoyed by a delay on our side."*
2. **Goal** — the effect the text should produce. *"Acknowledge the delay,
   reassure, keep the relationship."*
3. **Format** — the concrete shape expected. *"A short email, calm tone, no
   drawn-out apologies, 6 lines max."*

<Exemple>
**Poor request:** "Write an apology email." → the AI produces a generic, flat
text you'll have to rework entirely.

**Rich request:** "I run an accounting firm. A client is annoyed by a delay on
our side with their tax return. Write them an email that acknowledges the delay,
reassures them about what's next, and preserves the relationship. Calm,
professional tone, 6 lines max, no drawn-out apologies." → the result is usable
almost as is.

The second request takes thirty seconds longer to write and saves ten minutes of
rewriting.
</Exemple>

## When the result disappoints: adjust, don't give up

The first draft isn't always good — that's normal, and it's fine. The mistake
would be to rewrite it all by hand or conclude that "it doesn't work." The right
reaction is to **say what's wrong**, in one sentence:

- *"Too formal, make it warmer."*
- *"Too long, cut it in half."*
- *"Give me three versions instead of one, I'll choose."*

<Attention>
A common trap: starting a brand-new request from scratch each time, instead of
**refining the previous one.** The AI keeps track of the conversation: it's
almost always more effective to fix the text it just produced than to rephrase
everything. A well-aimed tweak beats a new request.
</Attention>

## A detail that changes everything: show an example

When you have a precise expectation about style, don't describe it — **show it.**
Paste a text you find successful and say: *"Write in this spirit."* AI imitates a
model very well. It's often faster and more faithful than trying to describe the
tone with adjectives.

## Capitalise: keep your good requests

<Idee>
A request that worked well is a **small reusable asset.** Keep it somewhere (a
file, a note). Next time, you'll only have to change the details. That's how you
go from "I improvise every time" to "I have my templates that run."
</Idee>

On the other hand, what you never reuse without looking are the **facts** the AI
produces: a figure, a date, a name still need checking every time (we saw this
in the previous lesson).

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Take a request you've already made to an AI — or invent one from your daily
work. Rewrite it by explicitly adding the **three ingredients**: context, goal,
format.

Then run it, and instead of stopping at the first result, make **a single
tweak** ("shorter", "warmer", "give me two versions"). Compare the two drafts.

The goal isn't perfection, but to feel the difference a careful request — and a
tweak — make.
</Exercice>

## In short

- A good instruction comes down to three ingredients: **context, goal, format.**
- When the result disappoints, **adjust in one sentence** rather than rewriting
  everything or giving up.
- For a precise style, **show an example** rather than describing it.
- Good requests are **kept and reused**; facts, on the other hand, are always
  checked.

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min recap (the context from lesson 1) · 5 min on
the three ingredients with the poor/rich comparison · 3 min on the tweak and the
example-as-model · 5 min live exercise on a case brought by a participant.

**Bring the example to life.** The most striking moment is the comparison "Write
an apology email" vs the rich version, live, on screen. Type both in front of the
group: the contrast speaks for itself. Ask a participant for a real case rather
than using your own.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Should I be polite with the AI (say hello, thanks)?"* → it changes nothing
  in the result; do as you feel. The content of the request matters, not the
  courtesy.
- *"How many times can I retry?"* → as many as needed; but if you're going in
  circles after 3-4 tries, it usually means the initial request lacked context.
  Go back to basics.

**Pitfall to avoid when teaching:** don't turn the lesson into a catalogue of
"prompt techniques." The goal is the context/goal/format habit, not a list of
tricks to memorise. Three ingredients, that's all.
</Formateur>

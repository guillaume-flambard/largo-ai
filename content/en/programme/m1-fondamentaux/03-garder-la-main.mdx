We now know what AI does well (lesson 1) and how to ask it well (lesson 2). What
remains is the most important habit, the one that makes all the difference
between calm use and risky use: **staying in control.** AI proposes; you decide,
and you stay responsible.

## The key idea

<Idee>
AI is an **assistant, not a decision-maker.** It produces raw material — often
good, sometimes wrong, always stated with confidence. Your role isn't to redo
everything, but to **reread, check what matters, and make the call.** The last
word is yours, and so is the responsibility.
</Idee>

This isn't a tedious constraint tacked on at the end: it's exactly what lets you
use AI *fast and at ease*. When you know you'll reread, you can let the AI move
quickly on the draft.

## Check what matters (and only that)

Not everything needs the same level of checking. The right balance:

- **Always check** — the **facts**: numbers, dates, names, quotes, references,
  amounts, legal articles. That's where AI invents without warning.
- **Glance over** — the **tone and meaning**: does it really say what I think, in
  the right register, without a misreading?
- **No stakes** — the **form**: spelling, structure, flow. AI is reliable here.

<Attention>
The danger isn't that AI gets it wrong — it's that it gets it wrong **with
confidence.** A false figure arrives in a perfectly built sentence, a false legal
article carries a credible number. The elegance of the text is never proof of
accuracy. The more sure-of-itself an answer looks, the more automatic the habit
of checking the facts should be.
</Attention>

## A concrete example

<Exemple>
You ask the AI for a letter that cites "article L1234-5 of the Labour Code." The
text is flawless, the number looks real. **Before sending:** thirty seconds to
check that this article exists and really says what the letter claims. That's
exactly the kind of detail AI can invent — and the kind of mistake that would
cost you dearly in front of a knowledgeable reader.

Conversely, if the AI rephrases your paragraph to make it clearer, without
introducing any new fact: a reread is enough, nothing to "check."
</Exemple>

## Deciding stays human

Checking the facts isn't enough. On anything that commits you — a choice, a
trade-off, a delicate message, a decision that affects people — AI can
**inform**, list options, give an opinion; but **it doesn't decide for you.** It
has neither your knowledge of the ground, nor your responsibility, nor the
consequences to bear. Use it as an adviser you listen to, not a boss you follow.

## What you don't hand over (for now)

<Attention>
Avoid pasting **sensitive or confidential data** into a consumer AI: clients'
personal information, medical data, company secrets, credentials. Depending on
the tool, what you enter may be stored or reused. When in doubt, anonymise
(replace real names and figures with examples) or leave it out. The detail of the
rules — GDPR, AI Act, choosing tools — is the subject of a dedicated module (M5);
here, we simply keep the reflex of caution.
</Attention>

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Take a text the AI produced for you recently (or have one produced on a topic
from your work). Run it through three mental columns:

1. **To check** — list each fact (number, date, name, reference) before using it.
2. **To reread** — do the tone and meaning really suit you?
3. **The decision** — what, in this text, stays *your* choice and not the AI's?

If you find no fact to check, the task was probably purely about writing — and
that's perfectly fine. What matters is that you looked.
</Exercice>

## In short

- AI **proposes**, the human **decides** and stays **responsible.**
- Check the **facts** first; tone gets a reread, form can be trusted.
- The **confidence of a text isn't proof**: the more sure-of-itself, the more you
  check.
- Avoid handing **sensitive data** to a consumer AI (details in M5).

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min transition (we know how to ask, now we stay in
control) · 4 min on the dosage of checking (facts / tone / form) · 3 min on
"deciding stays human" + sensitive data · 5 min live critical-reread exercise.

**Strong demonstration.** If the setup allows, trigger a hallucination live: ask
for a precise quote or a legal reference on a niche topic, then check it
together. Seeing the AI invent a credible number marks the group lastingly —
far more than an abstract warning.

**Frequent questions**
- *"How do I check without spending hours?"* → only check the facts that carry
  stakes. A harmless internal email doesn't need the same care as a legal letter.
  Common sense sets the dose.
- *"Couldn't the AI check itself?"* → asking it "are you sure?" sometimes helps
  surface a doubt, but doesn't replace checking at the source. It can confirm an
  error with the same confidence.

**Mindset to pass on:** take the drama out without trivialising. The goal isn't
fear of the tool, but *clear-eyed* confidence — we use it a lot, keeping our eyes
open.
</Formateur>

You now know what AI can do, how to ask it, and how to stay in control. What
remains is turning these habits into a **routine.** Many people try AI once, find
it impressive… then never come back, because they never tied it to their real
work. This lesson is here to prevent that.

## The key idea

<Idee>
You don't "adopt AI" all at once. You install **one small use at a time**, on a
real task that comes up often. Once that use feels natural, you add another. It's
the accumulation of small, regular gains — not the grand overhaul of automation —
that changes a way of working.
</Idee>

## Spotting your good starting points

Not all tasks are equal to begin with. The best candidates combine two qualities:

- **They come up often** — even a small gain, multiplied by frequency, becomes a
  real one.
- **They're mostly writing or formatting** — that's AI's playground (see
  lesson 1).

<Exemple>
A few typical starting points by trade:

- **Tradesperson / shopkeeper** — replying to quote requests, writing a listing,
  preparing a follow-up message.
- **Independent professional** — rephrasing a report, summarising an exchange,
  preparing a standard letter.
- **Small team** — writing a job description, structuring meeting notes,
  rephrasing an internal communication.

The common thread: frequent, low-stakes tasks where a quick draft saves time.
</Exemple>

## Starting small, on purpose

<Attention>
The temptation, after a good first experience, is to want to "run everything
through AI" at once. That's the surest way to spread yourself thin, get
discouraged, and introduce errors where the stakes are high. **One use, well
installed**, beats ten cobbled-together ones. You expand once the first has
become a reflex.
</Attention>

Pick **one** task this week. Just one. Do it with AI every time it comes up,
applying the habits from the previous lessons (context/goal/format, then check).
Let it become a routine before adding another.

## Measure to lock it in

What turns a trial into a habit is **seeing the gain.** No need for a dashboard:
the first time you do a task with AI, note in one line how long it took, and
compare to your "before" estimate. Two effects:

1. You spot where AI **really** helps you — and where it changes nothing, or even
   slows you down. Not every task is worth the detour.
2. The concrete gain makes you want to keep going, and to extend it to a second
   task.

<Idee>
The goal isn't to "have the machine do the work," but to **free up time** on the
repetitive so you can reinvest it where you're irreplaceable: the relationship,
the judgement, the craft. AI takes the draft; you keep the value.
</Idee>

## Your turn

<Exercice>
In two minutes, lay out your starting plan:

1. **One task** that's frequent and mostly about writing, which you'll do with AI
   this week. Write it down in black and white.
2. **The trigger** — the precise moment it comes up ("every time a client asks
   for a quote", "every Monday for the report").
3. **The measure** — how you'll know it was worth it (time saved, comfort,
   quality).

Next week, you'll know whether this task deserves to stay… and you'll be able to
pick a second one.
</Exercice>

## In short

- You adopt AI **one task at a time**, not all at once.
- The best starting points are **frequent and about writing**, low-stakes.
- **Starting small is a choice**, not timidity: a well-installed use calls for
  others.
- You **measure the gain** to lock in the habit and spot where AI really helps.

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 2 min module synthesis (understand → ask → check →
integrate) · 4 min on spotting tasks + examples by trade · 3 min on "starting
small" and measuring · 6 min exercise: each participant lays out their task for
the week, shares it aloud.

**Land the module.** This is M1's closing lesson: people should leave with a
**concrete commitment** each, not a vague intention. Have everyone say their
chosen task aloud — stating it in front of the group counts as a commitment. Note
them down if you'll see the group again: the next session starts with "so, that
task?".

**Adapt to the trades present.** Generic examples fall flat if the group is
homogeneous (all tradespeople, all professionals). Prepare two or three starting
points specific to their sector before the session; ask them for theirs too.

**Frequent questions**
- *"What if my task isn't about writing?"* → start with a writing task anyway to
  build the habit; more advanced uses (automation, connecting to tools) come in
  module M4.
- *"How long before it feels natural?"* → a few repetitions are enough for a
  simple use. What matters isn't the duration, but the regularity.

**Pitfall to avoid when teaching:** don't oversell. Promising hours saved per day
creates disappointment. A modest but real gain, repeated, is more convincing —
and more honest — than a promised revolution.
</Formateur>

Before opening any tool, let's set a clear baseline. Most bad surprises with AI
come from a misunderstanding at the start: we expect something it was never
built to give. Once that misunderstanding is cleared up, everything gets
simpler — and far less stressful.

## The key idea

<Idee>
A generative AI (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini…) is a **machine that produces
plausible text**, not a machine that tells the truth. It learned to predict
"the most likely next word" from a huge amount of writing. So it's excellent at
**rephrasing, structuring, drafting** — and unreliable as soon as **precise
facts** are involved (numbers, dates, quotes, references).
</Idee>

Put it in your own words: *it's not a search engine, nor a calculator. It's a
very gifted writing assistant that speaks with confidence even when it's wrong.*

## Where it shines

- Turning a rough idea into clear text.
- Rephrasing, summarising, translating, changing tone.
- Suggesting angles, outlines, first drafts to correct.
- Explaining a concept in your words, at your level.

## Where to be careful

<Attention>
It **confidently makes up** what it doesn't know — this is called a
"hallucination". A revenue figure, a law, a date, a quote, a client name: all of
these can be invented while looking perfectly credible. The rule is simple:
**whatever can be checked must be checked.**
</Attention>

## A concrete example

<Exemple>
**Good request:** "Write a polite follow-up email for a quote sent two weeks
ago, professional but warm tone." → the AI is in its element; the result will be
useful from the first draft.

**Risky request:** "What was our revenue in 2023?" → the AI doesn't know your
figures; it may invent one, plausible and wrong. The right approach: **give it**
the figure, and ask it to do something with it.
</Exemple>

## How to phrase a good request

Three elements turn an average result into a useful one:

1. **Context** — who you are, who you're talking to. *"I'm a carpenter writing
   to a private client."*
2. **Goal** — what the text should achieve. *"Follow up without putting them
   off."*
3. **Format** — the expected shape. *"A short email, 5 lines max."*

## Your turn

<Exercice>
Take a real writing task you have at hand (an email, a report, an announcement).
Hand it to the AI with **context + goal + format**. Then spot two things:

1. **One right thing** you'd keep as is.
2. **One thing to check** before sending (a fact, a number, a name).

Five minutes is enough. The goal isn't a perfect text, but to build the right
habit: *use, then verify.*
</Exercice>

## In short

- Generative AI produces **plausible**, not **true**: excellent on form,
  unreliable on facts.
- Use it to **draft, rephrase, structure** — not to know your own data.
- The habit that changes everything: **give context**, then **check what
  matters**.

<Formateur>
**Run-through (≈ 15 min).** 3 min framing (the starting misunderstanding) · 4 min
on the key idea + examples · 3 min on context/goal/format · 5 min live exercise
on a participant's real case.

**Frequent questions**
- *"Does my data go to the vendor?"* → point to module M5 (Security, GDPR & AI
  Act); reassure without going into detail here.
- *"Which tool should I pick?"* → don't settle it now; the habit matters more
  than the tool at this stage.

**Pitfall to avoid when teaching:** don't chain impressive demos. The goal of
this lesson is the **mindset** (use then verify), not performance. A real
exercise beats a spectacular demo.
</Formateur>
